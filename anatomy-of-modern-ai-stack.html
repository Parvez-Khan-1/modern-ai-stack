01 · The Hook · 0–10 mins

The Gap Nobody Tells You About

Academic AI vs Production AI — they are not the same subject

Let me start with a question. How many of you have trained a machine learning model? Probably most of you. Now — how many of you have deployed one to a million real users? That silence is the gap we're here to talk about.

Your curriculum is excellent at teaching you how models work. Gradient descent, backpropagation, cross-entropy loss — you understand these. But there is an entirely different discipline that sits between a trained model and a real-world AI product. Nobody teaches it in a classroom. You learn it by shipping things and watching them break at 2am.

The hard truth

In production, your model is the least of your problems. The data pipelines, the latency, the hallucinations, the cost, the monitoring — that's where engineers live.

I've spent 10 years on the production side of this. At Mastercard, I oversee 700+ predictive models running live. I've seen beautiful Jupyter notebooks fail spectacularly in the real world — not because the model was wrong, but because everything around it wasn't engineered properly.

Today I'm going to show you what that "everything around it" looks like. Not theory. The actual stack.

04 · LLMs & SLMs · 25–35 mins

LLMs and SLMs — What They Actually Are

No math. Just the right mental model.

A Large Language Model is, at its core, a very well-read autocomplete. It has read a significant portion of the internet, learned the statistical patterns of human language, and became extraordinarily good at predicting what word comes next — at scale, with context, and with surprising coherence.

But here's the critical limitation nobody talks about enough: LLMs are frozen in time. They were trained on data up to a cutoff date. They know nothing about your company's internal documents. They know nothing about your hospital's patient records. They know nothing about what happened last week.

This is where the distinction between LLMs and SLMs (Small Language Models) becomes important — not just academically, but as an engineering decision you'll face in your career.

LLM
Massive, powerful, expensive
GPT-4, Claude, Gemini Ultra
Billions of parameters
⟶
Best for
Complex reasoning, creative tasks, nuanced understanding. When accuracy matters more than cost.
SLM
Compact, fast, deployable
Phi-3, Mistral 7B, Llama 3.2
Millions of parameters
⟶
Best for
Latency-sensitive tasks, edge deployment, cost at scale. When you need 10M responses a day.

At Mastercard, this is a real engineering conversation we have for every AI feature: does this need a large model, or can a smaller, fine-tuned model do this job cheaper and faster? The answer changes everything — cost, latency, infrastructure, and compliance.

05 · RAG · 35–45 mins

RAG — How Industry Makes LLMs Work with Real Data

Retrieval Augmented Generation — the idea that bridges the gap

So we have a problem. LLMs are powerful but frozen. Your organisation has private, proprietary, real-time data that the LLM has never seen. How do you bridge this?

The naive answer is fine-tuning — retrain the model on your data. This sounds right but is almost always wrong. It's expensive, slow, and you'd need to retrain every time your data changes. In a bank, data changes every second.

The industry answer is Retrieval Augmented Generation — RAG. The concept is elegant in its simplicity:

R
Retrieve

Search your private data store for the most relevant chunks of information related to the user's query

A
Augment

Attach those chunks to the user's prompt — give the LLM a "technical manual" before asking it to answer

G
Generate

The LLM answers using only the provided context — grounded, accurate, and specific to your data

Think of it like an open-book exam. Without RAG, the LLM sits the exam with only what it memorised during training. With RAG, you hand it the exact chapter of the textbook it needs — right before it answers. The LLM's job becomes reading and reasoning, not memorising.

The engineering insight

The LLM is not the hard part of a RAG system. Finding, cleaning, chunking, embedding, and retrieving the right data is where real engineering happens. This is why strong fundamentals matter.

The "Retrieve" step uses a Vector Database — which stores your documents as mathematical representations (embeddings) and finds the most semantically similar ones to the query. Not keyword matching. Meaning matching.

Problem
"The AI is hallucinating — making up answers that sound confident but are wrong"
→
Solution
RAG — give it a grounded technical manual before it answers. Constrain it to your data.
Problem
"The AI doesn't know about our latest product / policy / data"
→
Solution
Update the vector database — no retraining needed. Your knowledge base is separate from the model.
Problem
"The AI is too slow — users are waiting 8 seconds for a response"
→
Solution
Use a smaller model for retrieval, cache frequent queries, optimise chunk sizes.

Operating Systems

DBMS

Vector Databases are a new category of database. Your understanding of indexing, query optimisation, and storage directly applies to how we build the RAG retrieval layer.

Machine Learning

Embeddings — the core of RAG — are learned representations. Your understanding of neural networks and loss functions explains how embedding models are trained.

Software Engineering

Every layer of this stack is a microservice. Clean interfaces, separation of concerns, API design — the principles your SE course teaches are how this system stays maintainable.

07 · Live Demo · 45–55 mins

Building a Minimal RAG Pipeline

A working system in under 30 lines of Python

Theory is useful. A working system is better. Let's walk through a minimal but complete RAG pipeline — the same conceptual architecture used in production systems, simplified to its essential moving parts.

Google Colab Notebook
Minimal RAG Pipeline — Zero Setup Required

Open in your browser, run every cell, have a working RAG system in 10 minutes. No installations. No API keys needed.

Open Notebook →

The notebook walks through three stages — mirroring the R-A-G flow exactly:

Stage 1 — Ingest: Take a PDF (your own notes, a research paper, a policy document). Split it into chunks. Convert each chunk into an embedding using a sentence transformer model. Store in a lightweight vector store.

Stage 2 — Retrieve: Take the user's question. Convert it to an embedding using the same model. Find the top-3 most semantically similar chunks from your store. This is your context.

Stage 3 — Generate: Construct a prompt: [System: Answer only from the context below] [Context: chunk1, chunk2, chunk3] [Question: user's question]. Send to an LLM. Return the grounded answer.

Your 2-Year Runway

Three things to do this week. Not someday. This week.

No matter how advanced AI gets, every production-ready solution will always stand or fall on the strength of its engineering foundation. The students who understand the full stack — not just the model — are the ones who build things that survive contact with reality.

  • Build and deploy one RAG demo

    Use the Colab notebook. Swap in your own data. Push it to Hugging Face Spaces — it's free and public. You now have a live, deployed AI system on your resume. Not a Jupyter notebook. A deployed product.

  • Follow one practitioner, not one researcher

    Researchers publish papers. Practitioners ship systems and write about what broke. Find engineers at companies you admire on LinkedIn or X and read what they post. That's your real curriculum supplement.

  • Read one system design case study

    Engineering blogs from Uber, Netflix, LinkedIn, and Airbnb document how they built AI systems at scale. One blog post per week. By final year, you'll think differently from every other candidate in a placement interview.

The one thing to remember

AI is not magic. It's very clever plumbing. And the engineers who understand the pipes — not just the model — are the ones who build things that last.
